02 / Core Concepts

What is Chain-of-Thought?

Three ideas that will change how you prompt forever. Read each, then see how they connect.

A CoT Reasoning Chain
Problem
Write a campaign strategy for our new data feature
→
Step 1: Audience
Who feels the most pain around data silos? Data-heavy ops teams...
→
Step 2: Hook
What's the emotional core? Fear of looking uninformed in exec reviews...
→
Step 3: Proof
What evidence makes it credible? Customer quote about 3-hour report time...
→
Output
Grounded, auditable campaign strategy with defensible reasoning
🧠
Show Your Work
When you ask AI to reason step-by-step rather than jump to an answer, the quality of that answer rises dramatically. The model "thinks better" when it externalises its reasoning — just like a human does when writing through a problem.
🔍
Audit the Chain
Once reasoning is visible, you can catch errors before they compound. Did the AI make a false assumption about your audience in Step 1? Fix it there. Without CoT, that assumption silently contaminates the final output.
⚙️
Steer Mid-Flight
CoT lets you interrupt and redirect. "Your Step 2 assumes we're targeting SMBs — we're actually enterprise. Continue from Step 2 with that correction." This is the difference between delegation and collaboration.
03 / Live Examples

See it in the wild.

Two real scenarios from Jordan's world. Study the prompt structure, not just the output.

EXAMPLE 01
Competitive Positioning Analysis
Situation: Jordan needs to brief the CEO on how their product stacks up against two new entrants before a board meeting. She has a product one-pager and two competitor blog posts. Without CoT, the AI produces a generic comparison. With CoT, it reasons through each dimension before synthesising — and Jordan can see exactly where the logic is sound or shaky.
▸ Prompt Structure (Chain-of-Thought)
ROLE: You are a B2B SaaS competitive analyst. TASK: Analyse how [Product] compares to Competitor A and B. Reason through this step-by-step before giving any summary. STEPS TO FOLLOW: Step 1: Identify the 3 most important buyer criteria for this category. Step 2: For each criterion, rate all three products (1-5) with evidence. Step 3: Note any assumptions you're making and flag uncertainties. Step 4: Only then — write a 3-sentence executive summary. CONSTRAINT: Do not skip steps. Show all reasoning.

EXAMPLE 02
Campaign Audience Strategy
Situation: Jordan is planning Q3 pipeline campaigns and needs to identify which ICP segment to prioritise. She has CRM export data (anonymised), last quarter's campaign performance, and the product roadmap summary. A simple "who should I target" prompt produces a vague answer. A CoT prompt forces the AI to reason through evidence before recommending.
▸ Prompt Structure (Chain-of-Thought)
ROLE: You are a demand generation strategist. TASK: Recommend the single best ICP segment to prioritise for Q3. REASONING CHAIN: Step 1: From the CRM data, what patterns exist in closed-won vs churned accounts? Step 2: From campaign performance, which segments showed highest engagement? Step 3: From the roadmap, which segment benefits most from upcoming features? Step 4: Weigh these factors — identify conflicts, explain your trade-offs. Step 5: State your recommendation with a confidence level (1–10) and why.

04 / Agent Builder

Build your CoT agent.

Walk through each step to assemble a reasoning agent personalised to your marketing workflow. Click each step to expand.

1
Define the Agent's Role
System Prompt

Every agent starts with a system prompt that defines WHO it is and HOW it reasons. For a CoT marketing agent, the role anchors the thinking style.

You are a senior B2B marketing strategist with deep expertise in SaaS demand generation. When given any strategic question: 1. Always reason step-by-step before stating conclusions 2. Number each reasoning step explicitly 3. After each step, flag any assumptions you're making 4. Only write your final recommendation AFTER completing all steps
2
Define the Reasoning Template
Chain Structure

Design a reusable step template your agent always follows. This is your CoT scaffold — it forces structured thinking on every task.

REASONING_TEMPLATE = """ For every strategic task: Step 1 — CONTEXT: What do I know? What's missing? Step 2 — AUDIENCE: Who is this for? What do they care about? Step 3 — EVIDENCE: What data supports each option? Step 4 — TRADE-OFFS: What are the risks of each path? Step 5 — DECISION: Recommend with confidence score 1–10 """
3
Attach Your Context Documents
Grounding

Ground your agent's reasoning in your actual data. Without context docs, CoT reasoning is speculative. With them, it's analytical.

CONTEXT_DOCS = [ "ICP_profiles.pdf", # Define who you're selling to "campaign_performance.xlsx", # What worked / didn't in past "competitor_intel.md", # Where you win and lose "product_positioning.pdf" # Core value props and proof points ]
4
Add a Self-Check Step
Quality Gate

The final component: a self-audit instruction that forces the agent to check its own reasoning before finalising. This catches the most common failure modes.

SELF_CHECK = """ Before finalising your output, review your reasoning chain: - Did I make any assumptions in Steps 1-4 not supported by the docs? - Is my recommendation actually the logical conclusion of my steps? - What would change my recommendation? State this explicitly. If you find errors, correct them before writing the final output. """
✓ Agent Built

Your CoT marketing agent is assembled. In a live session, you'd deploy this in Claude Projects or as a custom GPT. The reasoning template + context docs + self-check = a dramatically more reliable AI collaborator.
