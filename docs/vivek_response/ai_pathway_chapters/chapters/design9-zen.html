Chapter Two
Core Concepts

Three ideas that
change everything

Understanding these three concepts is enough to have an informed conversation with any vendor, engineer, or board member about AI agents.

I
Agents Act, Not Just Answer
A regular AI tool responds and stops. An agent takes a sequence of actions — searching records, sending notifications, updating systems — to complete a goal. The shift from answering to doing is fundamental.
II
Agents Use Tools
Agents have access to defined tools: your EHR, your scheduling system, your notification service. They decide which to use and when, based on what they're trying to accomplish. That tool-use capability is the operational superpower.
III
Guardrails Are Non-Negotiable
In healthcare, agents must operate within strict permissions, maintain full audit trails, and escalate to humans before any consequential action. Effective governance is not optional — it is the product.

Chapter Three
Live Examples

The technique, in practice

Two scenarios from your operational context. Each is real, deployable, and currently in use at health systems of similar scale.

The situation
Discharge delays cost your health system ~200 bed-hours per week. The bottleneck is sequential coordination: pharmacy, transport, social work, and family notification each wait for the previous step. An agent runs all four in parallel from a single EHR trigger.
EHR discharge trigger
Transport roster
Family contact list
Agent instruction
Goal: On discharge order, coordinate all downstream steps in parallel. Step 1: Notify pharmacy. Check-in after 30 minutes. Step 2: Reserve transport (soonest slot + 90-minute buffer). Step 3: Alert social work if care flag present in record. Step 4: Send approved family notification SMS. Rule: On any step failure → escalate immediately to charge nurse.
The critical phrase is "on any step failure → escalate immediately." Without explicit escalation rules, agents silently fail in consequential ways.
The situation
Your float pool coordinator spends 40% of their shift manually checking schedules and calling staff for same-day gaps. An agent monitors continuously and initiates outreach — but every booking requires a human confirmation before it executes.
Scheduling API
Staff availability data
Escalation rules
Agent instruction
Goal: Monitor schedule for gaps 4+ hours in advance. Step 1: Check every 60 minutes for gaps in the next 8 hours. Step 2: Cross-reference float pool availability. Step 3: Offer shift to top 3 available staff (by seniority). Step 4: If no response in 20 minutes → escalate to supervisor. Rule: Never confirm a booking without human approval.
"Never confirm a booking without human approval" is the essential guardrail. It keeps the agent fast and autonomous while preserving human control over commitments.
